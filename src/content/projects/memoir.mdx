### The 5-Second Version

India's ₹40,000Cr online jewellery market treats gifting as an afterthought — every competitor navigates by product category, ignoring the anxious gifter and the guilt-laden self-purchaser. I led the end-to-end UX design for Memoir: conducted a **competitive audit of 8 D2C brands**, ran **8 in-depth user interviews** across gifters and self-purchasers, mapped **dual journey architectures**, and designed a mobile-first e-commerce experience built around emotional resolution — not product grids. The result: an occasion-first navigation system, a 3-step guided gift flow, and a dual-copy architecture that addresses two fundamentally different purchase psychologies.

## Overview

Memoir is a D2C 925 sterling silver jewellery brand I designed from zero. The Indian online silver jewellery market is dominated by well-funded incumbents competing on discounts and product categories — none of them own gifting as an emotional category.

Over two months, I led research, strategy, and end-to-end UX design — from competitive audits and user interviews through information architecture, interaction design, and a functional mobile-first prototype connected to Shopify's headless API. I collaborated with Alka (jewellery design, brand identity, packaging) and Nishu (engineering review, infrastructure).

## What We Were Up Against

> "I spent 45 minutes on CaratLane trying to find something for my girlfriend's birthday. I ended up buying flowers instead." — Rahul, 28, Software Engineer

Five core challenges defined our design mandate:

<ProcessStep number="01" title="Gifting Is an Afterthought in D2C Jewellery">
  Every competitor — Giva, CaratLane, Mia — navigates by product category: Rings, Necklaces, Bracelets. None designs for the anxious gifter who doesn't speak jewellery language and needs guidance, not options.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="02" title="Two Audiences, Two Fundamentally Different Anxieties">
  The gifter carries taste anxiety ("What if she doesn't like it?"). The self-purchaser carries guilt ("Should I really spend this on myself?"). Both arrive at the same site — but need completely different emotional journeys.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="03" title="Mobile-Only Purchase Context">
  85%+ of Indian e-commerce traffic is mobile. The anxious gifter is on his phone at 11pm, three days before her birthday. The entire purchase journey happens in a 390px viewport.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="04" title="Trust Deficit in Online Silver">
  Silver lacks the established trust signals of gold jewellery. Users worry about purity, hallmarking, and whether what they see online is what they'll receive.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="05" title="Commodity Positioning Trap">
  Silver jewellery is increasingly positioned as a commodity — discount-driven, trend-chasing, interchangeable. No brand owns the emotional narrative space.
</ProcessStep>

## Research — 8 Competitors · 8 Interviews · 2 Journey Maps

### Competitive Audit: 8 D2C Brands Analysed

I conducted a structured audit of 8 direct competitors across four dimensions: navigation architecture, gifting features, mobile experience quality, and emotional positioning.

| Brand | Navigation Model | Gifting Feature | Mobile Quality | Emotional Depth |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Giva** | Category-first | Basic gift wrap | Good | Low — discount-led |
| **CaratLane** | Category + filters | Gift cards only | Excellent | Medium — occasion pages |
| **Mia by Tanishq** | Category-first | None | Good | Low — trend-led |
| **Qudera** | Category-first | None | Basic | Low |
| **TDERA** | Category-first | Gift wrap option | Basic | Low |
| **Ornate** | Category + price | None | Medium | Low |
| **Gouri** | Category-first | None | Basic | Low |
| **YoTreasure** | Category + material | Gift message | Medium | Low |

**Key finding:** Not a single competitor designed a guided gifting journey. Gift features, where they existed, were bolt-on additions (gift wrapping, gift cards) — never integrated into the core navigation or product discovery architecture. CaratLane had occasion-based landing pages but buried them three clicks deep.

### User Interviews: 8 In-Depth Sessions

I conducted 8 semi-structured interviews (45–60 min each) — 4 with gifters (men aged 24–35) and 4 with self-purchasers (women aged 22–32). Recruited through personal networks and social media screening.

### Key Research Findings

**Pattern 1: Gifters suffer from choice paralysis, not scarcity**

> "I don't need more options. I need someone to tell me which one is right for her birthday." — Ankit, 26

6 of 8 participants described feeling overwhelmed by product grids. Gifters specifically wanted fewer, curated recommendations — not broader catalogues. The current D2C model assumes more choice = better experience. Our research showed the opposite for gifters: more choice = more anxiety = higher abandonment.

**Pattern 2: Self-purchasers need permission, not persuasion**

> "I added a bracelet to my cart twice and removed it both times. It felt indulgent. If someone had just said 'you've earned this,' I might have checked out." — Priya, 29

4 of 4 female participants described some form of purchase guilt when buying jewellery for themselves. The cultural conditioning — especially in Indian households — frames self-purchase of jewellery as indulgent. Standard marketing language like "Treat yourself" and "You deserve it" triggered scepticism, not confidence. Participants responded more positively to framing that positioned self-purchase as acknowledgment ("Choose yourself") rather than indulgence.

**Pattern 3: The unboxing experience matters as much as the product for gifters**

> "Half the gift is how it arrives. If it comes in a plastic pouch, the whole thing feels cheap." — Deepa, 31

5 of 8 participants mentioned packaging or unboxing in unprompted contexts. For gifters, the unboxing is the first moment the recipient experiences the gift — it carries the gifter's taste and intention. No competitor was designing for this moment digitally — showing the gifter what the recipient would experience.

### Journey Mapping: Two Parallel Architectures

Based on interview findings, I mapped two distinct emotional journeys that would share a single codebase but diverge at key decision points:

**Journey A — The Gifter's Arc:**
Anxiety ("Will she like it?") → Guided narrowing (3-step flow) → Confidence ("This is the one") → Gift personalisation (note card) → Unboxing preview ("She'll love how it arrives") → Purchase

**Journey B — The Self-Purchaser's Arc:**
Guilt ("Should I?") → Permission framing ("You've earned this") → Emotional connection (story, not specs) → Material reassurance (trust signals) → Self-acknowledgment ("Choose yourself") → Purchase

> The critical insight: these two journeys share the same product catalogue but need fundamentally different copy, navigation paths, and emotional scaffolding. This dual-architecture requirement shaped every design decision that followed.

## Design Goals — Five Core Mandates

Research findings translated directly into design goals:

<ProcessStep number="01" title="Occasion-First Navigation Over Category Navigation">
  Replace "Rings / Necklaces / Bracelets" with "New Beginnings / In Her Honour / Just Because" — meeting users where their intent starts, not where the inventory is organised.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="02" title="Guided Gift Flow That Reduces Choice to 3">
  Design a step-by-step flow that narrows the full catalogue to 3 curated recommendations based on relationship and occasion — eliminating choice paralysis.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="03" title="Dual-Copy Architecture">
  Every product carries two parallel emotional framings — one for the gifter's anxiety, one for the self-purchaser's permission-seeking — surfaced contextually based on the user's entry path.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="04" title="Unboxing as a Digital Product Feature">
  Show the gifter what the recipient will experience — making the unboxing ritual a browsable, swipeable part of the product page, not a post-purchase surprise.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="05" title="Mobile-First as the Primary Design Surface">
  Every component designed at 390px first. Desktop derived upward from mobile truth. Touch-first interactions, bottom-anchored CTAs, full-bleed imagery.
</ProcessStep>

## Information Architecture — Dual-Journey, Single Codebase

I designed the IA to branch based on a single initial signal — the homepage CTA the user taps — while sharing a common product and checkout layer:

1. **Homepage** — Dual CTA segmentation: "SHOP NOW" (self-purchaser path) / "FIND A GIFT" (gifter path)
2. **Gifter Path: 3-Step Gift Guide** — Relationship → Occasion → 3 Curated Picks (full-screen modal, one-handed mobile use)
3. **Self-Purchaser Path: Moment Collections** — Occasion-based browsing ("First Light," "In Her Honour") with self-purchase copy framing
4. **Product Page** — Shared surface with contextual copy (gifter hook vs. self-purchaser hook), gift toggle, story section, unboxing ritual
5. **Cart & Checkout** — Bottom-anchored drawer, gift badge, personalised note persistence through Shopify checkout

## Design Solutions — What I Built

### A. Homepage: The First Segmentation Signal

The homepage captures purchase intent before the user sees a single product. Two stacked CTAs — "SHOP NOW" (filled) and "FIND A GIFT" (outlined) — sit at the bottom of the hero viewport, reachable with one thumb.

This is the branching point. Every subsequent screen adapts based on which CTA was tapped — copy, navigation, product sorting, and checkout UI all respond to this single initial signal.

Above the fold, a horizontally scrollable trust strip communicates material credentials ("925 STERLING SILVER · BIS HALLMARKED · FREE SHIPPING ABOVE ₹1,999") so the rational brain is satisfied while the emotional journey begins.

![Memoir — Mobile-first homepage with dual CTAs for gifters and self-purchasers](/cs/memoir-mobile-hero.webp)

> We considered three homepage approaches: (1) a unified browsing experience with late-stage gifting options, (2) separate landing pages per audience, or (3) early segmentation via dual CTAs on a shared homepage. Option 3 won — it captures intent without fragmenting the brand, and keeps the entry point simple for both audiences.

### B. Moment Navigation Over Category Navigation

Instead of "Rings / Necklaces / Bracelets," I designed moment-based navigation: "First Light" / "In Her Honour" / "Just Yours" / "New Beginnings." Each moment card is horizontally scrollable on mobile — a man looking for "something for her birthday" finds this navigation intuitive. A woman browsing for herself connects emotionally with "Just Yours" before she's even seen a product.

The trust strip above anchors material quality, so the moment-based navigation doesn't sacrifice credibility.

![Trust strip and moment navigation — emotion over category](/cs/memoir-mobile-moments.webp)

### C. The 3-Step Gift Guide

This is the core gifter feature — born directly from the research finding that gifters suffer from choice paralysis. A full-screen, 3-step guided flow designed for one-handed mobile use:

- **Step 1: "Who is this gift for?"** — Partner / Sister / Friend / Colleague / Daughter / Someone Special. Each option is a full-width tap target.
- **Step 2: "What's the occasion?"** — Her Birthday / A New Beginning / Just Because / She Achieved Something / I Miss Her / I Want to Say Sorry
- **Step 3: "Our picks for you"** — 3 product recommendations scored by relationship × occasion match, tagged "BEST MATCH"

He didn't choose from 50 products. He was guided to 3. The site made the decision easy.

### D. Product Page — Dual Copy + Gift Toggle + Story

The product page is the shared surface where both journeys converge — but the experience adapts to each audience:

**Dual-copy architecture:** Each product carries parallel emotional framings. The gifter sees: *"She just started something brave. This ring tells her you noticed."* The self-purchaser sees: *"For the woman who just turned the page."* The correct variant surfaces based on the user's entry path.

**Gift toggle:** When activated, a handwritten note card appears inline — parchment background with deckled edges, paper grain texture, Caveat handwriting font. Below it, three pre-written message options for gifters who freeze. It sits inline in the mobile scroll flow — no modal, no popup. It's part of the experience.

**"The Story Behind This Piece":** Each product has a dedicated narrative section. The copy is product-specific, not generic — connecting the piece to the moment it's designed for.

<ImageRow images={[

> We considered three gift message approaches: (1) a modal popup, (2) a collapsible accordion, or (3) an inline card revealed by toggle. The inline approach won — on mobile, modals break scroll flow and accordions feel transactional. The parchment card feels like writing a note, not filling a form.

### E. The Unboxing Ritual — Anxiety Resolution Through Touch

This feature was directly born from research: 5 of 8 participants mentioned packaging importance unprompted. The section "How She'll Receive It" shows the gift recipient's experience in three swipeable steps on mobile:

1. **The Opening** — Ink-pressed linen box sealed with care
2. **The Narrative** — Hand-lettered story card inside the box
3. **The Reveal** — Sterling silver nestled in silk velvet

The gifter's anxiety about "what will she actually get?" is resolved not by describing the unboxing, but by letting them experience it through touch interaction — swipe to open, swipe to read, swipe to see the piece.

### F. Cart & Checkout — Gift Persistence Through Purchase

The cart drawer slides up from the bottom on mobile. Gifted items carry a "GIFT WRAPPED" badge. The gift message (written on the parchment card) persists as a cart line attribute through Shopify checkout — so the personalised note travels with the order all the way to fulfilment.

The checkout button anchors to the bottom of the viewport with the total always visible — no scrolling required to see what you're paying.

## The Dual Emotional Architecture — Implemented in Code

Each of the six launch products carries parallel emotional framing — `hook` (self-purchaser) and `gifterHook` (gifter) — surfaced contextually throughout the interface:

| Product | Type | Self-Purchase Framing | Gifter Framing |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The First Chapter** | Ring | "For the woman who just turned the page." | "She just started something brave. This ring tells her you noticed." |
| **Chosen** | Pendant | "She didn't wait for someone to choose her — she chose herself." | "For the woman who gives everything to everyone else. This one's just for her." |
| **Still Here** | Bracelet | "For the friend who stayed. Through the late-night calls, the miles." | "She's been there through everything. This says what you can't always text back." |
| **Unwritten** | Earrings | "For the girl on the edge of something new." | "She's graduating, moving, starting fresh. Your way of saying 'go get it.'" |
| **Brave & Bright** | Ring | "For the woman who walked into the room and didn't shrink." | "She's been brave lately. This ring says you see her." |
| **The Quiet Promise** | Pendant | "Some promises don't need words." | "For the relationship that doesn't need grand gestures." |

**Language rules enforced across all copy:**
- Never "Treat yourself" (triggers guilt for self-purchasers)
- Never "You deserve it" without context (reads as marketing)
- Instead: "Choose yourself" — positions self-purchase as acknowledgment, not indulgence

## Visual Design System

**Colour palette:**
- `Ivory (#FAF7F2)` — not white (clinical), not cream (dated). Warm, paper-like.
- `Charcoal (#2C2C2C)` — not black (harsh), not grey (weak). Rich, readable.
- `Gold (#C9A96E)` — CTAs, hover states, moment labels. The brand's singular accent.

**Typography system:**
- **Cormorant Garamond** — editorial headings, product names. Literary, warm.
- **DM Sans** — body copy, UI elements. Clean, doesn't compete.
- **Caveat** — gift message card only. Handwritten, personal.

**border-radius: 0 everywhere.** Sharp edges on every component. Memoir is jewellery, not a SaaS product. The visual language should feel editorial and architectural, not friendly and rounded.

## Mobile-First Design Principles

For D2C in India, mobile isn't a viewport — it's the entire commerce context. This shaped every interaction:

- **Full-bleed product imagery** fills the viewport — no wasted margin, no sidebar, no grid. One product, one emotion, one screen.
- **Touch-first interactions** — swipe to browse products, swipe through the unboxing ritual, tap to toggle the gift card. No hover states as primary interactions.
- **Bottom-anchored CTAs** — "ADD TO CART" lives where the thumb naturally rests. The mobile cart bar shows item count and total without scrolling.
- **The gift guide modal** fills the screen as a full-sheet. Each step is a full-screen decision, not a cramped overlay.
- **Desktop derived upward.** The mobile experience scales up to desktop, not the other way around.

## Collaboration & Build Process

### The Team

This was a three-person team, each with a clear domain:

- **Me (Ashish)** — UX design, experience architecture, user research, frontend implementation. I owned the end-to-end user experience from research through shipping.
- **Alka** — Jewellery design, product photography, social media content, packaging design. She was the visual collaboration partner — the unboxing ritual section was designed in close collaboration with her packaging vision.

### AI as a Design Accelerator

I used AI tools to accelerate execution — not replace design thinking. The research, strategy, information architecture, and interaction design were entirely human-led. AI tools came in at the implementation layer:

**Claude Code** — After I'd designed the component specifications, interaction patterns, and emotional intent for each screen, I used Claude Code to translate those specifications into working React code. The strategic documents I'd written (30,000+ words) became the highest-quality prompts — because good UX specs and good AI prompts require the same clarity of intent.

**Gemini** — Generated placeholder product imagery and lifestyle shots during the build phase, allowing me to design and test the full experience without waiting for the photoshoot. Real product photography will replace these at launch.

**Sora** — Generated placeholder video content for the unboxing ritual sequence and hero carousel, enabling full experience validation before production shoots.

> "AI didn't design the gift guide flow or decide that moment-based navigation would outperform category navigation. The research did. The user interviews did. AI helped me build it faster — the same way Figma helps me design faster than pen and paper. The tool doesn't replace the thinking."

### The Stack Decision

The original plan used Figma Make + Supabase + Razorpay + Shiprocket — four tools, three backend integrations. Two weeks in, the integration overhead was consuming time that should have gone into the experience layer — where Memoir's actual differentiation lives. I made the call to collapse to Shopify headless (one API for payments, inventory, and orders) + Claude Code for frontend implementation.

This was a design decision, not a technical one: where does the team's limited time create the most user value? Not in payment integration. In the emotional experience layer.

## Key Design Decisions & Trade-offs

<ProcessStep number="01" title="Occasion-First IA Over Category-First">
  **The trade-off:** Users familiar with jewellery e-commerce expect category navigation. Moment-based navigation requires learning a new mental model. **Why we chose it:** Research showed that 6 of 8 gifters searched by occasion ("birthday gift"), not by product type ("silver ring"). For self-purchasers, moments created emotional connection before product exposure. We mitigated the risk with a trust strip that still communicates material and category information.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="02" title="'Send as Gift' Ships at Launch, Not Post-Launch">
  **The trade-off:** Shipping the full gift flow (guide + card + unboxing preview) at launch added ~3 weeks to the timeline. **Why we chose it:** If Memoir's entire positioning is gifting-first, launching without the gift flow is launching a different brand. The gift toggle, message card, and unboxing ritual ARE the brand — they had to work on mobile from Day 1.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="03" title="Inline Gift Card Over Modal">
  **The trade-off:** The inline parchment card adds vertical height to the product page scroll. A modal would keep the page shorter. **Why we chose it:** On mobile, modals break scroll flow and feel like form-filling. The inline card feels like writing a note — it belongs in the experience, not interrupting it. Usability testing confirmed users engaged more with the inline version.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="04" title="3 Recommendations, Not 6 or 12">
  **The trade-off:** Limiting the gift guide output to 3 products means some good options aren't shown. **Why we chose it:** Research showed choice paralysis was the gifter's primary barrier. 3 curated picks with a "BEST MATCH" tag made the decision easy. One participant said: "If you show me 3, I'll pick one. If you show me 12, I'll leave."
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="05" title="AI-Generated Assets as Prototyping Tools">
  **The trade-off:** AI-generated imagery can look uncanny and doesn't match real product quality. **Why we chose it:** These assets let us build and test the complete emotional experience — including visual storytelling — before the photoshoot. When real photography arrives, it drops into a proven experience architecture. The design decisions are validated before the expensive production step.
</ProcessStep>

## Impact & Validation

### Qualitative Validation

I ran informal usability sessions with 5 participants (3 gifters, 2 self-purchasers) on the functional prototype:

- **Gift guide completion:** 5/5 participants completed the 3-step flow without assistance. Average time: 38 seconds.
- **Gift card engagement:** 4/5 participants interacted with the parchment note card. 2 participants typed custom messages; 2 selected pre-written options.
- **Unboxing section:** 3/5 participants swiped through all three steps unprompted. One participant commented: "Oh, so this is what she'll actually get. That's nice."
- **Navigation comprehension:** 4/5 participants correctly identified moment-based navigation within 10 seconds. One participant initially looked for "Rings" before adapting.
- **Trust strip:** All 5 participants noticed the hallmarking and material information. One said: "I wanted to know it was real silver before I looked at anything else."

## Accessibility Considerations

- **Touch targets:** All interactive elements ≥48px tap area — critical for one-handed mobile use in transit
- **Colour contrast:** Gold (#C9A96E) on charcoal meets WCAG AA for large text; darker gold-text (#8B7340) used for body copy
- **Screen reader:** Proper labels, fieldsets, and ARIA attributes across the gift guide and checkout flow
- **Reduced motion:** Swipe interactions degrade gracefully to static layouts when `prefers-reduced-motion` is set
- **Multi-cue errors:** Colour + icon + text for all form errors — never colour alone

## Reflection

**Research before tools.** The competitive audit and user interviews took 3 weeks — nearly a third of the total timeline. But every subsequent design decision was grounded in real patterns, not assumptions. The occasion-first IA, the 3-recommendation limit, the inline gift card — all came from research, not intuition.

**Two journeys, one codebase.** The most architecturally challenging decision was designing two psychologically distinct experiences that share a single product catalogue and checkout flow. The branching happens at the copy and navigation layer, not the infrastructure layer — which meant the complexity was in the design, not the engineering.

**AI accelerates execution, not thinking.** The research, strategy, and design decisions were entirely human-led. AI tools compressed the build timeline — what would have taken a team of 3–4 engineers and a designer 3–4 months was built in ~2 months by one designer with AI leverage. But the leverage is in execution speed, not in design quality. The gift guide flow works because of what we learned from Ankit and Priya and Deepa in interviews — not because of what any AI tool suggested.

**Permission framing over persuasion framing.** The biggest copy insight: self-purchasers don't need to be convinced the product is good. They need permission to buy it for themselves. "Choose yourself" outperformed "Treat yourself" in every informal test. This reframing — from persuasion to permission — changed the entire tone of the self-purchaser journey.

**The unboxing is product.** Designing the unboxing ritual as a browsable digital feature — not just a physical packaging detail — turned out to be the highest-engagement section of the product page. It resolved the gifter's core anxiety: "What will she actually experience?" This was Alka's packaging vision brought into the digital product layer through close collaboration.
