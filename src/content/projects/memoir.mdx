## Context: The Problem Space

### The Market Gap

India's online silver jewellery market is dominated by well-funded incumbents — Giva (₹200Cr+ in funding), CaratLane (Titan-backed), Mia (a Tanishq sub-brand). They all compete on the same axis: trend-led designs, discount-driven acquisition, product-category navigation. Rings. Necklaces. Bracelets. Sale banners.

None of them own gifting as an emotional category. None of them design for the anxious boyfriend searching "what to gift her for her birthday." None of them acknowledge that a woman buying silver for herself might feel guilty about it.

### The Insight That Became the Architecture

> "People don't buy jewellery. They buy emotional resolution."

Two distinct buyers arrive at the same website — on their phones, during a commute or at 11pm — with two fundamentally different anxieties:

**The Gifter** carries taste anxiety — "What if she doesn't like it? What if it says the wrong thing about us?" He doesn't speak jewellery language. He doesn't want 50 options. He wants someone to tell him "this is the one."

**The Self-Purchaser** carries guilt — "Should I really spend this on myself?" She's culturally conditioned (especially in India) to spend on family, not herself. She needs permission, not persuasion.

This insight became the literal architectural foundation of every component I designed and coded — from the dual homepage CTAs to the copy system to the checkout flow's conditional UI. And because both buyers are overwhelmingly on mobile, every component was designed thumb-first, swipe-native, and optimised for a single-hand hold.

## Why Mobile-First Isn't a Responsive Strategy — It's the Product

For D2C in India, mobile isn't a viewport — it's the entire commerce context. Over 85% of Indian e-commerce traffic is mobile. The anxious gifter isn't browsing on a 27" monitor — he's on his phone at 11:47pm, three days before her birthday. The self-purchaser sees an Instagram ad and taps through. The entire purchase journey happens in a 390px viewport, often in a single session.

This shaped every design decision:

- **Full-bleed product imagery** fills the viewport — no wasted margin, no sidebar, no grid. One product, one emotion, one screen.
- **Touch-first interactions** — swipe to browse products, swipe through the unboxing ritual, tap to toggle the gift card. No hover states as primary interactions.
- **Stacked information hierarchy** — on mobile, you can't place price next to image next to CTA. Every element earns its vertical space or gets cut.
- **Bottom-anchored CTAs** — "ADD TO CART" lives where the thumb naturally rests. The mobile cart bar shows item count and total without scrolling.
- **The gift guide modal** fills the screen as a full-sheet, not a centred popup. Each step is a full-screen decision, not a cramped overlay.

Desktop wasn't ignored — it was derived. The mobile experience scales up to desktop, not the other way around.

### The Original Plan (Weeks 1-2)

</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="04" title="Shiprocket">
  Shipping integration — credential management, order tracking.

### What Broke — and What We Learned

**1. Figma Make couldn't hold the experience complexity.** Memoir isn't a standard product grid with filters. It has dual-audience journeys, conditional UI based on gifter/self-purchaser state, a multi-step guided flow, a gift message system, and emotional copy variants per product. The interactive logic — state management, conditional rendering, API stitching — had to be written by hand anyway.

**2. Supabase + Razorpay + Shiprocket = three integrations to build and maintain.** For a two-person team trying to launch in 90 days, maintaining three separate backend integrations was consuming time that should have gone into the experience layer — where Memoir's actual differentiation lives.

**3. Claude Code changed the economics of building.** I could describe the experience I wanted — the psychology, the interaction, the emotional intent — and get working React code that I could iterate on in real-time.

### The Decision: Collapse the Stack

**OUT:** Figma Make, Supabase, Razorpay, Shiprocket (4 tools, 3 backend integrations)

**IN:** Claude Code + Shopify (headless) + Vercel + GitHub + Firebase (1 AI engineering tool, 1 commerce platform, 1 hosting platform, 1 auth service)

> "In 2026, the cost of complexity isn't the tools themselves — it's the integration surface area. Every additional service is another set of credentials, webhooks, error states, and documentation to maintain."

## The Shopify Integration

Custom `memoir` metafield namespace — each product carries `hook`, `gifter_hook`, `story`, `moment`, `moment_label`, `specs`, and `gift_tags` as separate Shopify metafields. The `mapShopifyProduct()` function checks metafields first → falls back to a `PRODUCT_ENRICHMENT` map → falls back to raw descriptions. The site works at full emotional depth even when Shopify is partially configured.

Cart management via Storefront API mutations. Gift messages stored as cart line attributes (`_gift_message`), persisting through Shopify checkout. Checkout handoff constructs a URL with shipping address params encoded as query parameters, redirecting to Shopify's hosted payment page.

## Mobile Touch & Swipe System

Every image surface in the application is swipeable — designed for thumb-driven browsing:

**Product cards on the homepage** — Custom touch gesture handling with `onTouchStart`, `onTouchMove`, and `onTouchEnd`. A `touchDeltaX` ref tracks horizontal displacement. A 10px threshold distinguishes intentional swipes from accidental touches. A 40px threshold triggers the image transition. CSS `transform: translateX()` with `transition: transform 0.3s ease-out` for momentum-like movement.

**Product page gallery** — Full-width swipeable carousel with dx/dy threshold detection (horizontal swipes only — vertical scroll passes through). Dot indicators expand from 6px to 18px on active state, and a "Swipe for more" hint fades in after 1.5 seconds.

**The unboxing ritual section** — The three-step "How She'll Receive It" experience uses the same swipe pattern. Each step functions as a full-viewport swipeable card on mobile. On desktop, the three steps display in a grid — but the mobile experience is the primary design.

**Bottom-anchored mobile cart bar** — When items are in the cart, a persistent bar anchors to the bottom of the viewport showing item count, total, and a "VIEW CART" CTA. It never obscures the "ADD TO CART" button — the two occupy separate viewport regions.

## What This Project Demonstrates

### 1. End-to-end product creation with AI tools

One person created: strategy, experience architecture, visual identity, visual assets, 4,000+ lines of production code, backend integration, and deployment. No design agency. No photography studio. No engineering team. The entire product — strategy to live, mobile-first site — was created by one designer using AI as leverage across every dimension.

### 2. Mobile-first as a design philosophy, not a responsive afterthought

Every component was designed at 390px first. The gift guide is a full-screen sheet, not a centred modal. The product gallery is swipeable, not clickable. The cart is a bottom drawer, not a sidebar. Desktop adapts upward from mobile truth — not the other way around.

### 3. Psychological depth as a technical requirement

The dual copy system is implemented as parallel metafields in Shopify and conditional rendering throughout the component tree. The gift message card is a CSS artwork with 50+ polygon coordinates. The guided gifting flow narrows choice from full catalogue to 3 recommendations via relationship x moment scoring. Every design decision maps to a specific emotional mechanism, and every mechanism is implemented in working code.

### 4. Strategic documents as AI prompts

The 30,000+ words of strategy documentation became the highest-quality prompts for Claude Code. "The gifter's anxiety should decrease at this step" is simultaneously a UX specification and an engineering instruction. This is the compound return on document-first discipline: strategy documentation and AI prompting are the same skill.

### 5. The stack pivot as a design decision

Choosing to collapse from 4 tools to Claude Code + Shopify wasn't a technical decision. It was a design decision. Where does the team's limited time create the most user value? Not in payment integration. Not in shipping webhooks. In the emotional experience layer. The stack should serve the design ambition, not constrain it.

## Reflections & What I'd Do Differently

**I should have committed to the simplified stack earlier.** The weeks spent building toward Figma Make + Supabase + Razorpay could have gone straight into the experience layer with Claude Code + Shopify. The evaluation was valuable — but the answer was predictable in hindsight.

**The monolithic App.tsx should split before production.** It was the right call for build velocity with Claude Code (cross-component iteration is faster in one file), but needs proper file structure and code splitting before launch.

**Copy needs real user data.** Every emotional hook is currently my best hypothesis. The Day 90 copy will be better — informed by which framings correlate with actual purchases vs abandoned carts.

**The 48-frame product rotation should have shipped.** Infrastructure is built for 4 product types (ring, bracelet, earring, necklace — 48 frames each). Even with AI-generated placeholder images, the scroll-triggered interaction is worth testing for mobile engagement.

**AI-generated imagery is good enough to test, not good enough to launch.** Gemini and Sora produced visuals that let me build and validate the complete experience. But for launch, real product photography will replace every generated image. AI assets are prototyping tools for the visual layer, the same way Claude Code is a prototyping tool for the engineering layer.

## The Bigger Argument: What the Design Role Becomes

This project is my proof point.

The traditional product design workflow has clear handoff boundaries. Designer → Figma file → PRD → Engineer → QA → Ship. Each handoff introduces interpretation gaps, waiting time, and lost intent. Most design portfolios end at the Figma file.

In 2026, AI tools collapse every one of those boundaries:

- **Strategy → Documentation → AI prompts** are the same activity
- **Visual design → Asset generation** (Gemini, Sora) happens in the same session as layout decisions
- **UX specification → Working code** (Claude Code) happens in the same conversation
- **Frontend → Backend stitching** (Shopify Storefront API via Claude Code) happens in the same afternoon

The designer who can hold the full arc — from "why does this market gap exist" to "here's a live, mobile-first product that fills it" — doesn't replace engineers, photographers, or strategists. They collapse the feedback loop. The product gets better, faster, because there's no telephone game between intent and implementation.

The skills that matter in this model aren't pixel precision or code syntax. They're:

- **Problem framing** — identifying the right emotional problem to solve
- **Taste** — knowing when the parchment card feels like a note vs a form, at 390px wide
- **Systems thinking** — designing the dual-journey architecture, the data model, the fallback system
- **AI orchestration** — using the right AI tool for each dimension of the product
- **Strategic writing** — documents that align humans AND serve as AI prompts

This is the role I'm building toward. Memoir is the proof that it works.
