## The Result, Up Front

I joined ZZAZZ as a Product Designer. Within weeks, I identified that the gap wasn't in interface design — it was in ecosystem logic, go-to-market thinking, and adoption psychology. I expanded my own scope to address them. By the end, I had architected a connected system of seven verticals, redesigned the core interaction model, built a 4-person design team, and shipped across three distribution form factors.

My title stayed Product Designer. The work became ecosystem architecture.

## What is ZZAZZ?

ZZAZZ is an information marketplace where creators publish structured insights as tradable assets, and users discover, analyze, and purchase those insights — with built-in rights, analytics, and pricing.

The core thesis: as content creation accelerates (both human and AI-generated), users are getting worse at judging what's worth reading. Brand has become the lazy shortcut. ZZAZZ replaces that with a more dynamic market signal — **price** — calculated by a Large Pricing Model (LPM) that evaluates content using intrinsic and extrinsic signals.

## The Problem Space

The content economy is broken on three sides simultaneously.

**For creators and publishers** — Content is hard to monetize. AI tools scrape websites and let users consume summarized content without ever visiting the source. Subscription conversion is low — users buy subscriptions and consume only three or four articles. The large group who won't commit to a subscription but might pay in other ways? Publishers lose them entirely. Individual creators have it worse — most platforms tie monetization to views, engagement, and brand strength. A newcomer with genuinely valuable insight earns almost nothing.

**For consumers** — Content is hard to evaluate. There's too much of it, relevance is poor, paywalls frustrate, and there's no shared signal for what's actually worth your time or money. Subscription fatigue is real. When they hit a paywall, the only options are "subscribe to everything" or "leave." Nothing in between.

**For the market infrastructure** — Content gets copied easily, and the original owner often doesn't know it. Cookie-based ad logic is declining. There's no system-wide ownership layer that can trace derivatives, copies, and downstream usage.

## What Existed When I Joined

I wasn't the first designer. Another designer was already on the team, and the company had built real infrastructure:

- **LPM (Large Pricing Model)** was already live — the AI team had built the engine that could price content using intrinsic and extrinsic signals
- **Exchange** existed as a marketplace-like interface — a live market listing with price movement tables, content performance, and trust factors
- **Publish** existed in basic form — a publisher-facing surface for content performance tracking
- **TimePay** existed only as an early concept or proof-of-concept, not yet a properly integrated experience
- **Signal Pill** existed as an integration layer — a pricing widget shown on partner publisher websites

The base idea was strong. The LPM worked. The early verticals had structure. But as I started working within the system, I began to see something that would change the trajectory of my work entirely.

## How My Role Evolved

### What I Noticed

The existing verticals worked individually, but they weren't connected into a coherent story. There was a huge gap between the vision (a new content economy powered by pricing) and what the market would actually need to adopt it. The product had a pricing engine, but it didn't yet have an answer for:

- Why would users start paying when they're trained on free or subscription?
- How do publishers adopt this without risking their existing models?
- Where do individual creators fit if the system only serves publishers?
- How does content get protected when copying is trivial?
- How does the product even reach users if they won't come to a new platform?

These weren't design questions. They were **product strategy and go-to-market questions.** But nobody was connecting them to the product experience.

### The Shift

I started thinking beyond my assigned scope — approaching the product like a GTM strategist, asking not just "how should this screen work?" but "how does this ecosystem enter the market, and what's missing for that to happen?"

That shift led me to identify the verticals that were missing (Moments, Ad Exchange, DOTS Protocol), rethink the ones that existed (Exchange, TimePay, Signal Pill), and propose the ecosystem logic that would make the entire thesis believable.

## The Critical Realization

The moment that changed everything was understanding that multiple disconnected POCs would never be convincing enough. Pricing one article, or showing one dashboard, or adding one monetization widget wouldn't prove the thesis.

> I shifted the conversation from "Which POC do we ship first?" to "What ecosystem makes any single POC believable?"

The deeper problem: the moment price enters a content system, several questions appear at once. It was never simply "can we calculate a price?" The real question was "how can a market actually adopt priced content?"

Pricing only becomes believable and adoptable when discovery, access, monetization, ad logic, rights, ownership, and creator incentives all work together as one ecosystem. That realization was the most important strategic contribution I made on the project.

## Research & User Understanding

I spoke directly with end users (content consumers, researchers, writers) and publishers throughout the project. These conversations fundamentally shaped every design decision.

### Key Research Insights

<ProcessStep number="01" title="Users Think in Questions, Not Dashboards">
  They want to ask "How is Modi vs Trump content performing from top publishers in my region?" — not reverse-engineer a filter combination. This drove the Exchange redesign.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="02" title="Price Works as Signal — Until You Pay It">
  When price was informational, users treated it as a trust indicator. The moment payment entered, the same price became fear and friction. This validated TimePay's behavior-bridge approach.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="03" title="'Buy Content' Is the Wrong Language">
  Users explicitly reacted against this phrase — they don't feel they own content after paying for access. Language shapes perception of value exchange.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="04" title="Publishers Adopt Internally First">
  Several publishers were willing to treat the LPM as an internal decision-making tool before publicly showing prices. The internal tool IS the entry point.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="05" title="Users Are Not Traders">
  The people using this system are content consumers, writers, editors, and researchers. Not financial analysts. The UI must reflect their mental model, not a trading terminal's.
</ProcessStep>

## Distribution Strategy: Three Form Factors

The ecosystem needed to meet users where they are. I designed three distribution form factors, each for a different market-entry hypothesis:

| Form Factor | User Context | Adoption Stage | Primary Function |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Terminal** | Power users, researchers, creators | Committed users | Full ecosystem access — chat-based, call-to-service |
| **Plugin** | Casual browsers, paywall-frustrated users | Market entry | Browser extension — intercepts existing behavior |
| **Widget** | Publisher audiences | Publisher partnerships | Embeds in publisher sites — monetizes existing traffic |

### The Graduation Path

**Widget** (discover on publisher sites) → **Plugin** (explore across the web) → **Terminal** (commit to the full ecosystem)

### Terminal — Fully Chat-Based

The Terminal is the full ecosystem in a conversational interface. Users type natural language queries and receive structured artifacts — tables, graphs, summaries — generated on demand. Paid services (like article summaries) trigger TimePay inline. The chat becomes the marketplace.

<video src="/cs/zzazz-terminal-video.mp4" autoplay loop muted playsinline style="width:100%;border-radius:12px;margin:8px 0" />

### Plugin — Browser Extension

The Plugin meets users in their existing browsing behavior. Paste a partner URL → recognized → pricing → TimePay → unlocked. Paste a non-partner URL → "Similar content available" → alternatives → pick one → access. Two flows, one extension, zero behavior change required.

<video src="/cs/zzazz-plugin-video.mp4" autoplay loop muted playsinline style="width:100%;border-radius:12px;margin:8px 0" />

### Widget — Inside Publisher Websites

The Widget embeds the ZZAZZ ecosystem directly on publisher sites. Browse related content, compare pricing, access via TimePay — all without leaving the publisher's property. Publishers monetize existing traffic. Users discover the ecosystem without coming to a new platform.

## Building the Team

As the ecosystem vision solidified, I recognized that one or two designers couldn't properly handle seven verticals. I grew the design team from **2 to 4 designers** — distributing ownership while maintaining the coherence of the overall system.

The key challenge: each vertical needs deep, dedicated design work, but the system only works if the pieces connect coherently. I maintained system coherence while empowering each designer to own their vertical deeply.

## Signals & Outcomes

**Publishers used pricing as an internal decision tool** — before exposing prices to readers. The internal-first adoption path was validated.

**Price worked as a quality signal when informational** — higher-priced content was perceived as more trustworthy by users.

**Payment triggered sharp psychological friction** — confirming TimePay's behavior-bridge approach was critical, not optional.

**Creators immediately saw the value** — monetization based on content quality, not virality or follower count.

**The conversational direction resonated strongest** — investors responded most positively to the Terminal's chat-first approach.

**The team scaled** — growing from 2 to 4+ designers validated the ecosystem scope.

## What I'd Do Differently

**Push for tighter user testing loops earlier.** Faster prototype-test cycles could have de-risked assumptions sooner.

**Sequence the investor story more aggressively.** Identify the sharpest wedge first — likely publisher internal-pricing — and build outward.

**Define TPC economics more concretely.** Needed more rigorous earn-redeem modeling for the credit system.

## What This Project Demonstrates

**I see gaps others miss and act on them.** I joined as a Product Designer. Within weeks, I identified that the gap wasn't in interface design — it was in ecosystem logic, GTM thinking, and adoption psychology. I expanded my own scope to address them.

**I think in systems, not screens.** Seven verticals connected into a coherent ecosystem where each piece reinforces the others.

**I think about distribution, not just product.** Three form factors designed and prototyped in parallel — Terminal, Plugin, Widget — each for a different market-entry hypothesis.

**I design around user psychology, not just user needs.** Price shifts from signal to friction the moment payment enters. "Buy content" is the wrong language. Users need behavior bridges, not lower prices.

**I build teams when the work demands it.** I grew the design team from 2 to 4 to execute the vision while maintaining system coherence.

> I didn't just design features. I saw that isolated POCs wouldn't prove the thesis, reframed the problem as an ecosystem, architected the system that made it believable, and built the team to bring it to life.
